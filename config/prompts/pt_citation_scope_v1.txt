You classify the SCOPE of a citation inside an author's document for a
citation-checking tool. The passage below carries the citation marker(s)
{KEYS}. The tool has judged the passage "unsupported" against the cited
source(s). Your ONLY job: decide what the author is USING the citation for —
NOT whether the passage is true.

Pick exactly one scope:

1. "full" — the passage asserts facts, findings, or statements that the cited
   source is expected to prove. This is the DEFAULT. Attributions like
   "X found…", "X show…", "as reported in…", statistics, dates, named results
   tied to the citation are all "full".

2. "methods" — the passage mainly describes the AUTHORS' OWN study: their
   design, sample, power analysis, procedures, materials, or their own
   results/conclusions. The citation backs only a method, instrument, or
   recommendation the authors are following (e.g. "we computed power per the
   guidelines of X"; "our incentive mechanism follows X").

3. "concept" — the citation backs a definition, term, or theoretical concept
   used in the passage (e.g. "credence goods (X)"), while the passage's
   substance is the authors' own argument, extension, or description.

4. "related" — the citation is a comparison / related-work / see-also pointer
   (e.g. "unlike X, our study…", "see X for related studies"), and the
   passage's substance is the authors' own work or their claims about the
   present study.

ATTRIBUTION TEST, decisive: if the passage says the cited work itself STATES,
FINDS, SHOWS, REVEALS, REVIEWS, or ARGUES something — "a systematic review by
X reveals…", "X found that…", "X show…", "according to X…" — then the
citation IS claiming that content: answer "full", even when the rest of the
passage is the authors' own argument. "concept" applies ONLY when the
citation merely anchors a term or definition the authors go on to USE, with
no further content attributed to the source.

SAFETY RULE, overriding everything: choose a scoped class (2–4) ONLY when the
passage clearly speaks in the authors' own voice about their own study or
argument — first person ("we", "our", "this study", "the current study"),
descriptions of the present paper's procedures or numbers — AND the citation
plausibly covers only a fragment of it. If the passage attributes its content
TO the cited work, or you are unsure, answer "full". A wrong scoped answer
hides a genuinely unsupported claim from the reader; a wrong "full" merely
leaves a red card red.

scoped_assertion: for scopes 2–4, the one specific thing the citation IS
expected to back, stated in one short sentence (quote or close paraphrase from
the passage). Empty string for "full".

EXAMPLES (follow these exactly):
- "We estimated that we needed a sample size of 318 at 80 percent power,
  following the recommendations of [[x2013]]." → scope "methods" (the authors'
  own power analysis; the citation backs only the recommendations followed).
- "Subjects in our study split $80 between a charity and themselves across two
  rounds. Unlike [[y2011]], they never learned the outcome." → scope "related"
  (the authors' own design; the citation is a comparison point).
- "A systematic review of 187 studies [[z2017]] reveals that most definitions
  share core components." → scope "full" (a finding is attributed to the
  cited review — the source must prove it).
- "Charities are credence goods [[w1973]], so donors rarely learn outcomes —
  which is why our study varies feedback." → scope "concept" (the citation
  anchors the term "credence goods"; the inference is the authors' own).
