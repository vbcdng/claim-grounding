Whether foragers worked less than farmers is one of anthropology's most durable debates, launched by Marshall Sahlins's argument that hunter-gatherers were an "original affluent society" who, despite owning little, needed only two to four hours of work a day [@sahlins1972]. That claim rested heavily on Richard Lee's fieldwork among the Ju/'hoansi (!Kung); Lee's own later accounting, once food processing, tool-making, and housework were included, raised total work closer to a standard industrial week, a revision anthropologists still cite as a caution against the low original estimate. Fieldwork with the Ache of eastern Paraguay directly tested the foraging-versus-farming comparison within a single population: once families that had been full-time foragers settled at an agricultural mission, both men and women worked fewer hours each day than they had in the forest, ate less, and shifted their childcare and labor patterns [@hawkes1987].

Larger, more recent comparisons suggest the leisure gap tracks market exposure rather than subsistence mode alone. Analyzing 45,019 time-use observations from 863 adults across eight small-scale societies plus industrialized-country data, one study found shifting to fully commercial labor was linked to men's average work rising from about 45 to 55 hours weekly, while women's held near 55 hours regardless of market integration [@bhui2019]. Consistent with this, a study of the Agta of the Philippines found individuals in camps more engaged in agricultural and other non-foraging work had substantially less leisure, a difference driven mainly by women's changing time use [@dyble2019].

No single, universal hunter-gatherer work-hour figure exists to set against agricultural societies; totals depend on which activities are counted and how exposed a group is to markets. What looks fairly robust is that commercialization, more than farming itself, tends to lengthen the working day.
