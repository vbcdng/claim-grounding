The claim that human violence has declined across history is a prominent and contested thesis in criminology and conflict research, and it holds up unevenly depending on which kind of violence is examined. Working from centuries of European court and coroner records, Manuel Eisner documented a roughly tenfold-to-fiftyfold drop in homicide, from around 20–70 per 100,000 people in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries to about 1 per 100,000 today, with decline starting earliest in the affluent, urbanizing northwest before spreading elsewhere [@eisner2003]. Present-day figures fit a world that remains violent but far less lethal per capita than its past: the UN Office on Drugs and Crime estimated a global homicide rate of 5.8 per 100,000 in 2021, amounting to roughly 52 people killed every hour worldwide, exceeding combined deaths from armed conflict and terrorism [@unodc2023].

Whether this pattern extends to warfare is far less settled. Using interstate conflict data back to 1823, one statistical analysis found the post-1945 "long peace" cannot be reliably distinguished from an ordinary fluctuation within a stable, fat-tailed conflict process, and a separate extreme-value analysis of war casualties over two millennia reached the same conclusion; a genuine downward trend in war has never been firmly established [@clauset2018; @cirillo2016].

Evidence on prehistoric violence is contested too. Comparing war-group sizes and casualties across small-scale societies, historical and contemporary states, and the nations that fought the two World Wars, one analysis found that both group size and death tolls scale predictably with population, and that once this scaling is accounted for there is no reliable difference in conflict investment or lethality between small-scale and state societies [@oka2017].

The case for a real decline in violence is thus strongest for documented homicide within settled states, and weaker once generalized to warfare or projected into deep prehistory.
