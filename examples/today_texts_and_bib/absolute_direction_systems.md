---
title: "Cultures That Use Absolute Directional Systems Instead of Relative Left/Right"
bibliography: absolute_direction_systems.bib
---

Most well-described languages anchor spatial description in the speaker's body, using left, right, front, and back; a smaller set instead fixes description to the landscape or compass, an absolute or geocentric frame of reference [@Levinson1996]. The clearest case is Guugu Yimithirr, spoken around Hopevale in northern Queensland: the language has no ordinary word for left or right, and speakers describe even body-relative locations, such as an ant crawling on someone's leg, with cardinal-direction roots instead [@Levinson1997]. A review of Aboriginal astronomy reports Guugu Yimithirr speakers point to compass directions with an accuracy of roughly 13 degrees, a figure traced to Levinson's fieldwork [@Norris2016].

A similar but distinct system exists among Tzeltal Maya speakers of Tenejapa, Chiapas, where an absolute "uphill/downhill" axis running south to north, crossed by a sunrise-sunset axis, locates objects at every scale, and no projective left/right frame is in ordinary use [@BrownLevinson1993; @Brown2012]. In Bali, the geocentric axis kaja–kelod (mountainward–seaward) organizes spatial talk and settlement layout, and its mountainward pole carries ritual purity the seaward pole lacks [@WassmannDasen1998].

Whether speaking such a language reshapes memory and reasoning beyond speech, rather than reflecting shared reliance on landmarks, remains debated. Majid, Bowerman, Kita, Haun, and Levinson argue speakers' preferred frame is consistent across memory, gesture, and inference tasks, tracking the frame dominant in their language [@Majid2004]. Li and Gleitman countered that similar performance is induced in English speakers simply by removing distinctive landmarks, so a causal language effect has never been conclusively established [@LiGleitman2002]; Levinson and colleagues rejected that reanalysis point by point [@Levinson2002]. Comparative work with children and apes finds an inherited bias across all four great-ape genera toward environment-anchored coding, so culture may override an allocentric default rather than replace an egocentric one [@Haun2006].
