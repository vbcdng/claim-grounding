---
title: "Why Bohemia Never Produced a Holy Roman Emperor Before the Přemyslid Dynasty Died Out"
---

Bohemia's failure to place a Přemyslid on the imperial throne owed less to weakness than to a constitutional status that made its rulers electors rather than plausible candidates. In 1212 Frederick II's Golden Bull of Sicily confirmed hereditary kingship for Přemysl Otakar I and reduced Bohemia's tribute and military duties [@wihoda2023]. Decades later, the Sachsenspiegel's list of electoral princes omitted the Bohemian king from those voting first, on the ground that he was "not German," and philological analysis dates that clause precisely to the disputed election of Rudolf of Habsburg on 1 October 1273, when Ottokar II's proxy vote was set aside [@wolf2020]. That episode fits a wider pattern in which German identity hardened through contrast with Slavic neighbours, even though it was institutional weakness, more than settled nationhood, that made such rhetoric useful [@scales2012].

Political calculation reinforced this exclusion. By 1273 Ottokar ruled Austria, Styria, Carinthia and Carniola besides Bohemia and Moravia, the wealthiest prince in the empire, so electors chose the obscure Rudolf of Habsburg to check that dominance [@lexikonbayern2020; @habsburgerascent2020]. Princes whose autonomy rested on a weak crown had reason to resist a stronger successor: after Rudolf died in 1291 the electors bypassed his son Albert for Adolf of Nassau, explicitly to forestall a hereditary Habsburg dynasty [@habsburgeralbrecht2020]. Bohemian kingship also legitimized itself through dynastic and sacral tradition centred on Saint Wenceslas's cult, distinct from the German princes' elective idiom—though how far this shaped perceptions of Ottokar as an outsider remains interpretive [@antonin2017; @kalhous2023].

Ottokar's death at Marchfeld in 1278 settled the rivalry by force, and no later Přemyslid seriously contested an election before the line ended with Wenceslas III's assassination in 1306.
