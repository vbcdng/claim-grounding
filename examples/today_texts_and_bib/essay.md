# Did Habsburg family cohesion explain their imperial success?

The claim that the Habsburgs built their empire mainly because, unlike rivals, they avoided infighting and stuck together captures a real strategy but overstates its uniformity. Their signature method was intermarriage rather than conquest, and historians treat the dynasty's sixteenth-century marriage diplomacy as coordinated, family-wide statecraft rather than private romance [@fichtner1976]. That coordination relied on real cooperation: in the late 1560s Philip II worked with three Habsburg female relatives spanning Madrid, Vienna, Lisbon, and Paris to secure near-hegemony over the European marriage market, while Maximilian I's own marriages were shaped by wide social networks of kin and court officials [@roddy2023pillars; @lutter2024gendering].

Yet unity repeatedly broke down. Evans's standard account describes recurring partitions among quarreling brothers, from the 1379 Treaty of Neuberg through a three-way 1564 split among Ferdinand I's sons that took over a century to reunify [@evans1979making]. Rudolf II's brother Matthias led a revolt stripping him of most territories early in the seventeenth century, a rupture a dedicated volume of essays still treats as a genuine dynastic crisis, the "Bruderzwist," not a marginal quarrel [@buzek2010bruderzwist]. Charles VI's 1713 Pragmatic Sanction, overriding his nieces' claims for his own daughter, was legal machinery meant to forestall fraternal disputes, and it did supply the legal basis for unifying the disparate lands for over a century [@roider1972pragmatic].

Comparative research on 961 monarchs across 42 European states, 1000–1800, finds primogeniture reduced deposition risk generally, a stabilizing mechanism not distinctive to the Habsburgs [@kokkonen2014stability]. Whether Habsburg cohesion exceeded rivals' like the Valois has never been rigorously measured, and the evidence suggests it carried costs: geneticists estimate first-cousin unions cut offspring survival to age ten by 17.8 percentage points, contributing to the dynasty's 1700 extinction [@alvarez2009inbreeding]. Cooperation mattered, but the record is managed rivalry, not harmony.
