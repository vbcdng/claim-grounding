---
title: "Could the EU Realistically Integrate into a Single Powerful Economic Entity?"
---

The European Union built a continent-wide single market and, later, a shared currency well ahead of any matching political authority, and Martin Feldstein argued in 2012 that this design, not merely poor national policy choices, made a sovereign-debt crisis in the currency area almost inevitable [@feldstein2012]. Other scholars frame the same gap differently: European integration has repeatedly advanced only by "failing forward," as incomplete institutions produce crises that force further, still-incomplete integration [@jones2016].

Structural economics backs this diagnosis. An early optimum-currency-area index found the prospective eurozone met only some standard criteria for a workable monetary union, and later work traced the missing piece to the absence of a fiscal union able to absorb asymmetric shocks [@bayoumieichengreen1997; @degrauwe2013]. The labour-mobility shortfall often cited as proof of this may be less severe than assumed, though: comparative research finds migration absorbs a similar share of local demand shocks in Europe and the US, even as adjustment runs roughly twice as slowly in Europe [@beyersmets2015].

De Grauwe locates the deeper obstacle in politics, arguing eurozone governments have refused fiscal union because none will accept the automatic mutual liability a political union would demand [@degrauwe2013]. That refusal is a choice, not a law of nature, which is why "doomed from the start" overstates the case even as "easy" clearly understates it.

Crises keep forcing partial answers. In July 2020 the EU agreed to issue up to €750 billion in jointly guaranteed debt through Next Generation EU, a step read as a milestone toward fiscal integration that still left the union's architecture incomplete [@camousclaeys2020]. The IMF has separately argued that without deeper fiscal risk-sharing the currency area will keep facing existential vulnerability [@imf2018].
