---
title: "\"Knock on Wood\": Real Origin"
bibliography: knock_on_wood.bib
---

Ask why people knock on wood and the usual answer reaches for tree worship: Celts and Germanic peoples supposedly believed spirits lived in sacred oaks, spirits a knock could rouse for protection. The paper trail does not reach that far. The standard folklore reference reports no attestation of ritual wood-touching before 1805, when it appears in a children's chasing game rather than a rite, putting the silent gap since England's Christianization at roughly 1,200 years [@simpsonroud2000].

In games such as "tiggy touchwood," a player was safe from capture only while touching wood, and folklorist Steve Roud argues this rule of sanctuary better explains "touch wood" than any memory of tree spirits [@roud2010]. Both sources agree the practice is far too recent to be a pagan survival [@simpsonroud2000; @roud2010], a conclusion etymology supports, dating "touch wood" citations to only a little over a century ago [@liberman2015]. The habit outliving its imagined origin is unsurprising, since rituals for managing chance rarely need a real mechanism, only a feeling of control.

Evolutionary biologists have modeled why misattributed causality persists, showing selection can favor assuming a link between unrelated events when a wrong guess is cheap and an occasional right one pays off well [@fosterkokko2009]. Psychology gives the gesture a measurable effect: across five experiments, people who "tempted fate" and knocked wood or threw a ball away from their body judged a jinx less likely than those moving identically toward themselves [@zhangrisenhosey2014]. Risen's account of magical thinking suggests educated adults can knowingly reject a superstition's logic yet still act on it, calling this acquiescence [@risen2016].

Performance benefits are less settled: one cited study reported a "lucky" golf ball raised putting success from 48% to 65% [@damisch2010], but higher-powered replications found lucky-ball participants performing almost identically to controls, so the effect was never firmly established [@calinjageman2014].
