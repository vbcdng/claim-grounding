---
title: "Why Do Old Pots End Up on Fence Posts? Rot Prevention and the Ghost of Vessel Magic"
bibliography: bibliography.bib
---

Across Britain, the Low Countries, and rural Europe, an upturned kettle, teapot, or flowerpot capping a wooden fence post draws little local comment. No single study documents this exact custom, so any explanation borrows from better-studied traditions: timber maintenance, and Europe's practice of using vessels for household protection.

Wood science supplies the mundane reading: decay fungi need moisture, oxygen, warmth, and a food source together, and the exposed end grain atop a post is where rainwater collects longest [@usda_wood_handbook]. Capping that grain with a waterproof vessel is a cheap repair, and the habit plausibly outlived its structural rationale to become decoration.

A second, older thread runs through what archaeologists call apotropaic house protection, a cross-culturally recurring practice of placing charged objects at a dwelling's vulnerable edges [@walker2022]. Early modern English witch bottles were stoneware or glass jugs filled with pins, nails, and bodily fluids, then heated and concealed near hearths and thresholds, attested from roughly the 1650s to at least 1705 [@thwaite2020]. The popular claim that these bottles were preventive charms barring witches is not well supported by the surviving texts, which frame them chiefly as a cure applied once bewitchment was already suspected [@thwaite2020]. A related Finnish tradition of hiding animal bones, coins, and tools in thresholds shows the same anxiety, with witchcraft fears more pronounced in densely built towns than in looser settlements [@hukantaival2016]. English museum surveys report a comparable concealment pattern at hearths and doorways [@historicengland2016; @mola_witchbottles].

None of this proves a gatepost pot was ever more than a cheap cap. Its survival owes less to remembered ritual than to overlap between thrift and a folk vocabulary treating old crockery as charged with meaning.
