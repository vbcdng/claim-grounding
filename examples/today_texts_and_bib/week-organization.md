The seven-day week is widespread today, but it is a historical accident rather than a natural time unit, and calendrical systems built on other cycles recur across unrelated societies [@zerubavel1985; @hill1966]. In West Africa, periodic trade produced the dominant alternative: rural markets rotating on cycles of three, four, five, or eight days, with four- and eight-day rotations especially widespread in the forest zone and the seven-day pattern largely confined to areas near Islamized trading centers [@hill1966]. Among the Akan of Ghana, an indigenous six-day cycle combined with an imported seven-day one into a 42-day *adaduanan* cycle, within which four days were marked inauspicious for farming or burial [@bartle1978].

Comparable systems arose independently elsewhere. Republican Rome structured public life around an eight-day nundinal market cycle that Romans, reckoning inclusively, called a nine-day cycle, and it remained central to Roman time-consciousness even after Constantine adopted the seven-day week officially [@ker2010]. In Mesoamerica, the 260-day tzolk'in ritual count, built from 20 named days permuted against 13 numbers, still guides Highland Maya daykeepers; its earliest securely dated instance, a "7 Deer" glyph from San Bartolo, Guatemala, has been radiocarbon-dated to between 300 and 200 BCE [@stuart2022].

Two modern states tried to abolish the week outright. Revolutionary France's 1793 calendar replaced it with a ten-day décade, moving the rest day from every seventh to every tenth day [@vera2009]. The Soviet 1929 *nepreryvka* split industrial workers into five rotating shift groups so factories never idled, decoupling social rhythm from Sunday [@schwarz1931]. Contemporary accounts judged the scheme workable in production terms but socially disruptive: the evidence suggests staggered rest days made shared family time hard to coordinate, and the policy was abandoned within a decade [@schwarz1931]. That such disparate societies converged, unprompted, on non-weekly rhythms suggests the seven-day week is a durable habit, not an inevitability.
